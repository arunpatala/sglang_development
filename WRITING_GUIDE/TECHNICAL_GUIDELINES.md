Overall main guidelines (technical teaching + technical books)
1. Know one reader, not “everyone.”
Define 1–2 learner personas: prior knowledge, goal (ship code vs. pass an exam vs. build mental models), and time budget. Novices need models and guided paths; experienced readers need gaps filled and tradeoffs—one volume rarely serves both equally.

2. Separate learning, doing, machinery, and understanding.
The Diátaxis frame is widely used because it reduces mixed modes (which creates fatigue): tutorial (first success), how-to (task recipes), reference (precise facts), explanation (why / mental model). A “book from scratch” usually needs all four, but in different chapters or tracks, not blended sentence-by-sentence.

3. Fight the curse of knowledge.
Experts skip “obvious” steps and pack jargon. Teaching writing explicitly plans where confusion lands (wrong intuitions, index mistakes, hidden side effects) and checks drafts with real beginners or structured reviewers.

4. Manage cognitive load on purpose.
Evidence-based instructional design (e.g. Mayer / Clark) stresses: remove clutter, segment long chains, signal what matters, align text with visuals/code in time and space, and avoid redundant channels that compete for attention (e.g. long code + dense prose saying the same thing twice without adding insight).

5. Prefer “productive success” over encyclopedic coverage.
Especially for code-heavy books: narrow outcomes per chapter (“after this, you can predict X / build Y”) and repeat with variation (spiral), rather than dumping full APIs.

6. Test the material like software.
Workshops, short courses, blog series, or early access readers catch what is unlearnable on the page. Iterate; Chapter 4 often forces rewrites of Chapter 3.

7. Voice and empathy are pedagogical tools.
Conversational framing (still precise), explicit “first read / second read,” and check questions turn passive reading into practice—this aligns with generative-processing ideas in multimedia learning (engage the reader in thinking, not only consuming).

8. Books are long-lived products.
Plan for feedback loops (errata, exercises, a repo), and for the fact that publishing logistics (proposal, scope, updates) are a separate skill from explanation—worth reading author-focused guides if you intend traditional publishing.
